Sessions

Types of Sessions

SIG meetings will be geared to a particular style of learning:

  1. Lab: The general objective of this type of session is to develop and practice a skills related to using devices through experiential (i.e., "hands on") learning. The session facilitator will provide exercises and assist others as needed to complete the exercises. Participants need to be able to bring their digital devices with them to these types of sessions.
  2. Lecture: The general objective of this type of session is to increase participants' understanding of select/specific digital technologies.
  3. Discussion: The general objective this type of session is to explore how select/specific digital technologies impact/influence culture and society.

In terms of SIG meetings that we schedule during our Spring 2026 term (i.e,. between January 23 and April 3), the priority will be on Lab and Lecture (with the intention of more Lab and Lecture only as needed/desired by the group). In choosing the themes/topics for sessions during the Spring 2026 term, priority will be on themes/topics covered in the "Tech Skills 101: Driver's Ed for the Digital World" course conducted in the Fall 2025 term.

Structure of Sessions

OLLI staff has generously reserved a room for this SIG to use every other Friday starting January 23, 2026. The room is reserved from 9:00 until noon these days. Participants in the Fall 2026 "Tech Skills 101" course who are interested in participating in this SIG had a fairly strong "Woah! That's a long time for a meeting!" response when I originally shared the room booking dates/time. Fair enough feedback, so I have given some thought to how we get the most out of this time without forcing participants to give a full half day of their time to participate in a meeting.

I will plan the content for each meetings chosen theme/topics so that it can be reasonably completed in 90 minutes (give or take). With the remaining portion of time to help folks one-on-one if any attendees want individualized assistance related to the meeting's theme/topics or other issues/challenges they would like help with. I am currently thinking of using the "backend" of our meeting times kind of like a college professor's office hours. I.e., I'll be there to help any SIG member with whatever they would like assistance with. Note, there is an implied "to the best of my ability" related to providing others with this type of help.

Proposed Sessions

Files

This session's primary aim is to increase participants' proficiency accessing and managing files via hands on exercises. Exercises will encompass standard/typical tasks we do with our devices' file manager apps, apps that allow you to create/save new files and open existing files, and working with files stored locally on your device's disk drive as well as in a cloud drive.

User Interfaces

This session's primary aim is to increase participants' proficiency "reading the screen." We will review common user interface components (e.g., windows, menus, toolbars, controls, etc.). Exercises will be designed to give you the opportunity to practice identifying user interface components on your screen, understanding what the controls do, as well as using them.

Browsers

Browsers are an important kind of app when it comes using the Internet; it is hard for me to imagine that everyone doesn't use a browser at times. And while all browsers have the same primary purpose (i.e., to visit/view content found on websites on the Internet), they also have common "convenience" features, security features, and privacy features. In this session we can review what these common features are and do some exercises to familiarize ourselves with how to use them. Some of these common features are

  • bookmarks
  • search
  • extensions
  • privacy and security
  • syncing
  • profiles
  • history

I also think we can review some meaningful user interface concepts while "deep diving" into browsers and doing so could help us better determine if we want to add a session to our schedule to "dive" deeper into general user interface concepts.

Password Managers

During the safety/security sessions in our Fall 2025 course, we spoke very briefly about password managers. I get a lot of value from using a password manager, and want to enable you to do the same. I attempted to limit discussion of password managers in our Fall 2025 course to creating basic/general awareness of them. Like many of the other topics we explored during our Fall 2025, there is more complexity to choosing a password manager and effectively using it than we could reasonably cover in the time we had for our security sessions.

Apple introduced a password manager app (called "Passwords") with iOS 18 and macOS Sequoia and I started using it more extensively (primarily to experiment with passkeys). Given we all have at least one Apple device, I believe we can do some worthwhile/interesting exercises using Apple's Passwords app that could help you either start using this app yourself or better enable you start to use some other password manager if Apple's doesn't suite your tastes/needs.

Safety: Cybersecurity

In the Fall 2026 "Tech Skills 101: Tech Skills 101: Driver's Ed for the Digital World" course, we had two sessions on this topic. The primary objective of this SIG meeting are to practice via lab exercises skills associated with securing our accounts and data. This could include using a password manager, creating strong passwords, using an authenticator app for two factor authentication, and using Apple's advanced cloud security features.

Settings

Settings are an important kind of software feature; they allow you to control/customize how your device and/or your apps. In my experience, it's fairly common for the cause and/or solution to problems we have with our devices to be related--in part if not entirely--to a system or app setting.

In this session, we will review the myriad available settings and learn how to differentiate between system settings and app settings.

The Manual

Participants in the "Tech Skills 101: Driver's Ed for the Digital World" course held during the Fall 2025 term expressed a lot of interest in having a single reference (i.e., a book) they could turn to when they needed assistance using their devices. Unfortunately, there is no single book that explains everything we can do with our devices. Instead there are many books, and finding the best book(s) for you can be challenging (for a variety of reasons). Regardless, the passion of folks interest in this kind of reference prompted me to spend time thinking about how "computer geeks" tend to address this kind of need and I think there are some strategies/methods used by "computer geeks" that anyone could learn/practice that fill this need.

Email

Email is a key feature of the Internet; everyone uses email. While we briefly touch on email in some session in the Fall 2026 "Tech Skills 101" course, we didn't have a focused discussion on much less any kind of "deep dive." It may be useful/worthwhile to schedule a SIG meeting on email. Possible topics we could cover in such a session:

  1. How to manage spam email
  2. How to manage the storage space consumed by your email service
  3. How to identify/recognize scam email
  4. How to use an email app to manage your email (instead of using your browser)
